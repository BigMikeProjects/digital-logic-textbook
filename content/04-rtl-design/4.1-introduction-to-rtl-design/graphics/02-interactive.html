In Practice
Experienced Designers

Working engineers typically go straight to HDL — writing Verilog or VHDL directly.

They have internalized the separation of data and control through years of practice. The RTL structure lives as a mental model in their thought process — RTL diagrams and the FSMD approach can be circumvented.

The FSMD

A Finite State Machine with Datapath (FSMD) captures the "algorithm" — what computation happens and when it happens — in a single diagram. This approach works best for relatively simple systems.

States contain register transfer operations. Transitions depend on inputs and/or data conditions.

Key Insight: The FSMD mixes data and control together. Our design methodology will separate them into distinct hardware units.
Key Concept: Separation
Every RTL system decomposes into two cooperating parts
⚙️
Controller
Sequences the operations
  • FSM determines current step
  • Generates control signals
  • Reads status from datapath
  • Decides what happens next
⇄
⚡
Datapath
Performs the computation
  • Registers store data
  • Adders perform arithmetic
  • Muxes route data
  • Comparators test conditions
The datapath supports the computations. The controller sequences them and drives the datapath.
This separation is an organizing principle of RTL design.

1
Write the FSMD
Capture the algorithm as a state machine with register transfer operations annotated on each state. This is your specification.
2
Construct the Datapath
Extract every register, operation, and data routing need from the FSMD. Connect registers, adders, muxes, and comparators.
3
Design the Controller
Build a traditional FSM that generates the control signals your datapath needs — mux selects, register enables, operation codes.
4
Compose the Circuit
Wire the controller outputs to the datapath control inputs. Connect datapath status signals back to the controller.
